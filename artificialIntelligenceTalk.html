Dr. Nikolaus Kriegeskorte is a professor of neuroscience and a Principal Investigator and Director of Cognitive Imaging at the Zuckerman Mind Brain Behavior Institute at Columbia University. He uses deep neural network models, a form of AI, to mirror the brain’s visual system. Over the years, Dr. Kriegeskorte and his team have been improving their systems to mimic biological processes with greater accuracy. Moreover, these AI models not only simulate vision system but also other types of intelligence, such as language, reasoning, and motor control.

Once we understand vision, we can understand something more general: How the brain makes inferences

  • A neuron has many input signals and a single output (binary)
  • A neural network model abstracts from what a neuron does
    • Model "neuron" --> "Unit"
  • The way a unit works is by taking in all the inputs, multiplies each by some weight, and compares to some constant. This then decides the output
  • This is called a perceptron

  • Perceptrons don't work the greatest, so now we have multi-layer perceptrons

  • To train the network, we input a lot of pairs of images with the correct classification.
  • If the connection gets it wrong, it wiggles weights a little to better classify the images
  • The image above is just a cartoon. Real deep networks have millions of units, and they're huge

Transformation of representational similarity

  • When we compare cats and dogs, a cat might be just as different from another cat than a dog
  • If you show people one image at a time and measure their brain activity pattern in each region, you can attempt to decode what the person saw.
  • Something they found is that the brain patterns for similar images (faces, etc.), they tend to have similar brain activity patterns
  • Depending on the area of the brain, you can group images by type (face, etc.) or you can distinguish between 2 different human faces
  • One thing they were able to find was the brain grouping all faces (humans, dogs, cats, monkeys, etc.) as animates, separate fro inanimates
  • Humans group other humans far fro each other because it's important for us to distinguish between humans. We then group monkeys close together because it's not very important for us to be able to identify 2 different monkeys; they're just monkeys to us
  • Similarly, monkeys pair human faces together because they don't care a lot about the differences, whereas they pair monkey faces apart so as to better distinguish their own kind
  • One problem with neural networks is that you build the model, train it, and then you don't understand how it really works
  • Some scientists say this is kinda useless because you still don't know how it works
  • However, because the neural networks model brains, it is much cheaper to use models than humans. It can cost thousands to get one person to sit down and look at pictures and map their brain activity. Doing the same with machines is much cheaper
  • I can't show it here, but after training networks with either animals and objects, or human faces, you can back-propagate and form cool, psychedelic-looking images which portray (to some degree) the model is looking for at that layer

A child sees an escalator for the first time...

  1. Ooh, stairs!
  2. Those are some nifty railings
  3. Huh. Why are those people moving?
  4. ...

After imagining and interpreting and merging this new information with knowledge about the world, the child categorizes the escalator in their brain (then later maps that to its name after they're told the name)

We now understand

  • how the child recognizes people, handrail, diagonal upward motion, and scene context

We don't understand how the child

  • infers the goal of the people and the function of the escalator
  • imagines riding on it
  • forms the new concept "escalator" from a single experience (before learning the word), and instantly recognizes it thereafter
